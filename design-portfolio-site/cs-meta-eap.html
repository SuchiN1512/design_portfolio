A 12-week Early Adopter Program for Business AI (India)

Embedded with 8 real Indian SMBs over 12 weeks to de-risk a global Business-AI launch — logging 86 issues, benchmarking usability, and surfacing the #1 retention risk before rollout.

What kind of design this was

Longitudinal design research that drove product redesign

This was longitudinal design research and usability evaluation with a strong service-design lens — studying how an AI agent lives inside real businesses' daily operations over time, not in a one-off test. As sole owner, I designed the program, benchmarked usability against industry norms, ran issue triage with cross-functional partners, and produced the evidence that triggered redesigns and reset product priorities ahead of a global launch.

Context & problem

Is the AI agent ready for India?

Meta's Business AI agent was preparing for scaled global launch. Before investing in broad rollout, the team needed to de-risk adoption and prevent churn by understanding how real businesses use the agent in their day-to-day operations over a sustained period.

India was a critical market — WhatsApp is the dominant business channel there — but Indian SMBs have unique characteristics: heavy reliance on paid click-to-chat ads for leads, broadcast messaging as a core commerce channel, multi-language environments (Hindi, English, regional), and high ROI expectations from paid tools.

The program had to answer: Is the AI ready for India? What breaks? What delights? And what will cause businesses to churn before they ever get to monetization?
The challenge

Real businesses, real stakes, 12 weeks

  • Sole ownership of a multi-month program: session planning, issue tracking, stakeholder updates, vendor management, and adaptive design — all while running other projects.
  • Real businesses with real stakes: participants used the AI with their actual customers; when it failed (hallucinated language, blocked broadcasts, dropped paid leads) they lost real revenue — making sessions emotionally charged.
  • Volume & velocity of issues: 8 businesses × daily use × 12 weeks meant issues accumulated fast (86 total) and needed robust triage and routing.
  • Cross-market pattern recognition: findings had to be mapped against parallel programs in other markets to separate India-specific from systemic.
  • A moving target: the underlying AI model updated mid-program, so issues resolved or appeared between sessions — requiring adaptive tracking.
My approach

A designed 6-session arc

I recruited 8 diverse businesses across e-commerce and lead-gen, advertiser and non-advertiser, so findings would represent the breadth of Indian SMB use cases — then designed a session arc moving from onboarding to monetization, each session building on the last, with a maintained issue tracker for systematic triage.

Synthesis & communication

  • Identified the #1 retention risk — revenue-channel integrity: when the AI disrupts paid leads or blocks broadcasts, businesses see direct revenue loss.
  • Benchmarked usability against industry norms with standardized scoring, and tracked issue evolution across sessions (persistent vs. emerging).
  • Maintained a consistent stakeholder-update format (TL;DR → what we did → what we found → what we're doing about it) and coordinated critical-issue triage with PM, Design, and Engineering.
  • Synthesized cross-market patterns by connecting India findings with parallel programs.
Designing the human–AI interaction

Diagnosing — and reshaping — the agent's behavior

This wasn't only usability evaluation; it was a sustained read on how an autonomous agent behaves inside a real business, and what that behavior does to trust:

  • Trust-breaking behavior: language hallucination read as a scam by end customers — an interaction failure requiring a model-level fix, not a UI tweak.
  • Behavioral guardrail: defined the interaction minimum bar — the agent must never silence a paid lead or block a broadcast (the revenue channel is sacred).
  • Agent-instruction model: too many overlapping ways to "teach" the AI (Rules / Example Responses / Sources / Ask Customer Info) — argued for one coherent instruction model users can reason about.
  • Non-deterministic, moving target: designed tracking for an agent that changed mid-study — distinguishing fixed vs. persistent vs. newly-emerged behaviors.
Why it's design, not just bug-finding: framing scattered failures as one "revenue-channel integrity" guardrail turned them into a single launch-blocking product principle adopted across markets.
AI-native practice. This is designing for agentic AI — benchmarking agent behavior, tracking model evolution, and setting behavioral guardrails. For designing with AI (vibe-coded, interactive HAI prototypes), see the multi-agent belonging case study.

Evidence that shaped launch readiness

#1
retention risk surfaced pre-launch
  • Revenue-channel integrity = the minimum bar for market readiness — the AI must never silence a paid lead or block broadcast.
  • A below-norm usability score on the Rules flow triggered a redesign — research objectivity ("vs. industry norm") drove the decision.
  • Perceived value dropped across early sessions not because AI quality was bad, but because the AI disrupted existing money-making workflows — pointing to a fundamental product rethink.
  • Language hallucination was perceived by customers as a scam risk — an immediate trust destroyer requiring a model-level fix.
  • Configuration complexity (too many overlapping AI-teaching options) signalled a need for simplification.
  • Cross-market validation: the revenue-channel risk recurred across multiple markets — confirming a systemic product gap, not a local one, and strengthening the case to fix it as a pre-launch blocker globally.

Reflection

What I learned

What worked: structured issue tracking with severity tiers made prioritization easy for cross-functional partners; a consistent update format built trust over 12 weeks; and benchmarking against industry norms gave findings objectivity ("below norm" is more compelling than "users struggled").

What I'd do differently: build a mid-point stakeholder alignment to re-prioritize remaining sessions; escalate the highest-severity paid-lead issue earlier; and add lightweight async check-ins between sessions to keep participants engaged.

What I learned: how to run a longitudinal program end to end as sole owner; the difference between issue severity and prevalence; how to tell a "moving-target" product story when the AI improves mid-study; and the power of cross-market triangulation.
